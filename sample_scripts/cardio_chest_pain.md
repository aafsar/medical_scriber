# Cardiology Consultation — Test Script

> **Scenario:** 55-year-old female referred for evaluation of exertional chest pain over the past 2 weeks.
> **Expected duration:** ~3-4 minutes
> **Key terms to verify:** echocardiogram, ejection fraction, troponin, electrocardiogram, atorvastatin, amlodipine, dyspnea, palpitations, murmur, systolic, diastolic, coronary artery disease, hyperlipidemia, metoprolol

---

**Doctor:** "Good afternoon, I'm Dr. Rivera, one of the cardiologists here. I understand Dr. Thompson referred you for some chest discomfort. Tell me what's been going on."

**Patient:** "Hi, doctor. For the past two weeks, I've been getting this pressure in my chest when I exert myself — like when I walk up a hill or climb stairs. It wasn't there before."

**Doctor:** "Can you describe the sensation more specifically? Is it sharp, or more of a pressure or tightness?"

**Patient:** "It's more like a pressure, right in the center of my chest. It doesn't radiate anywhere, but it's definitely uncomfortable."

**Doctor:** "How long does each episode last?"

**Patient:** "Maybe three to five minutes. It goes away when I stop and rest."

**Doctor:** "On a scale of zero to ten, how severe is it?"

**Patient:** "About a five out of ten. Enough to make me stop what I'm doing."

**Doctor:** "Do you get any shortness of breath or dyspnea along with the chest pressure?"

**Patient:** "Yes, I do get a bit short of breath when the pressure comes on."

**Doctor:** "Any palpitations — a racing or irregular heartbeat?"

**Patient:** "Occasionally I feel like my heart flutters for a second, but it passes quickly."

**Doctor:** "Any dizziness, lightheadedness, or fainting episodes?"

**Patient:** "No, nothing like that."

**Doctor:** "Any nausea, sweating, or jaw pain with these episodes?"

**Patient:** "No."

**Doctor:** "Has it ever happened at rest, or only with exertion?"

**Patient:** "Only with exertion so far."

**Doctor:** "Let me ask about your medical history. What other conditions do you have?"

**Patient:** "I have high cholesterol — hyperlipidemia, they called it. And high blood pressure. Both for about five years."

**Doctor:** "What medications are you on?"

**Patient:** "Atorvastatin forty milligrams at night for cholesterol, and amlodipine five milligrams once daily for blood pressure."

**Doctor:** "Any allergies to medications?"

**Patient:** "No known drug allergies."

**Doctor:** "Do you smoke?"

**Patient:** "I quit about ten years ago. I smoked about a pack a day for fifteen years before that."

**Doctor:** "Alcohol use?"

**Patient:** "A glass of wine with dinner, maybe two or three times a week."

**Doctor:** "What do you do for work?"

**Patient:** "I'm an accountant. Mostly desk work."

**Doctor:** "Family history — any heart disease in your family?"

**Patient:** "Yes, my father had a heart attack at age sixty. My mother has high blood pressure."

**Doctor:** "That's important to know. Any other symptoms I should know about? Fever, weight changes, leg swelling, cough?"

**Patient:** "No, none of those."

**Doctor:** "Let me examine you now. Your vitals today: blood pressure is one forty-two over eighty-eight, heart rate seventy-two and regular, respiratory rate sixteen, temperature ninety-eight point six, oxygen saturation ninety-eight percent on room air, and your BMI is twenty-seven point one."

**Doctor:** "You appear well, no distress. Let me listen to your heart. I hear a regular rate and rhythm. There is a soft grade two over six systolic murmur at the apex, without radiation. No gallops, no rubs."

**Doctor:** "Lungs are clear to auscultation bilaterally, no wheezes or crackles. Abdomen is soft, nontender. No peripheral edema in your legs. Your pulses are strong and symmetric."

**Doctor:** "I reviewed the electrocardiogram that was done in the office today. It shows normal sinus rhythm, no ST changes, no Q waves. PR interval and QT interval are normal."

**Doctor:** "We also drew labs. Your troponin is negative, which is reassuring — that means there's no acute heart muscle damage. Your total cholesterol is two twenty, LDL is one forty — still a bit high despite the atorvastatin. Your hemoglobin A1c is five point seven, so no diabetes."

**Doctor:** "Based on everything — your age, the exertional chest pressure with dyspnea, your risk factors including the smoking history, hyperlipidemia, hypertension, and your father's early coronary artery disease — I'm concerned about possible angina. The negative troponin and normal electrocardiogram are reassuring that this isn't an acute event, but we need to work this up further."

**Doctor:** "I also note the soft systolic murmur, which could be functional but I want to characterize it."

**Doctor:** "Here's my plan. First, I want to order an echocardiogram to assess your heart's structure and function — specifically the ejection fraction, valve function, and wall motion. Second, I'm going to schedule you for a stress test to evaluate for ischemia under exertion. Third, I'd like to increase your atorvastatin from forty to eighty milligrams daily to get your LDL below one hundred. Fourth, I'm going to start you on metoprolol succinate twenty-five milligrams once daily — this will help control your heart rate and may reduce the chest pressure episodes. Continue your amlodipine as well."

**Doctor:** "I also want you to keep a log of your symptoms — when the chest pressure happens, what you were doing, how long it lasted. If you experience chest pain at rest, pain lasting more than ten minutes, severe shortness of breath, or you feel like you're going to faint, go to the emergency room immediately."

**Doctor:** "I'll see you back in two weeks after we have the echocardiogram and stress test results. We'll decide then if you need any further workup like a cardiac catheterization. Do you have any questions?"

**Patient:** "Will the stress test be safe if I'm having chest pain?"

**Doctor:** "Good question. We'll monitor you closely during the test. Your electrocardiogram is normal and troponin is negative, so you're stable for a stress test. We can stop the test at any time if you have symptoms. It's important for us to see what happens under controlled conditions."

**Patient:** "Okay, that makes sense. Thank you, doctor."

**Doctor:** "You're welcome. My office will call you to schedule the echocardiogram and stress test. Take care."
