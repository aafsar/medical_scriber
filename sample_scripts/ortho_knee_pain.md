# Orthopedic Consultation — Test Script

> **Scenario:** 58-year-old male referred by PCP for 3-week right knee pain. Based on masterplan Section 3.3 sample note.
> **Expected duration:** ~3-4 minutes
> **Key terms to verify:** meniscus, McMurray, Lachman, osteophyte, effusion, crepitus, diclofenac, metformin, lisinopril, antalgic, osteoarthritis, medial joint line, Kellgren-Lawrence

---

**Doctor:** "Good morning, I'm Dr. Patel from orthopedic surgery. I see you were referred by Dr. Sarah Chen. What brings you in today?"

**Patient:** "Hi doctor. My right knee has been hurting for about three weeks now. It started kind of gradually and it's just been getting worse."

**Doctor:** "I'm sorry to hear that. Can you point to where exactly you feel the pain?"

**Patient:** "It's mostly on the inside part of my knee, right along here."

**Doctor:** "The medial side, okay. And you said about three weeks — did anything happen that triggered it? A fall, a twist, any injury?"

**Patient:** "No, nothing like that. I used to work in construction, retired about two years ago, and it just kind of started on its own."

**Doctor:** "Understood. How would you describe the pain? Sharp, dull, aching?"

**Patient:** "It's more of a dull ache most of the time. Sometimes it gets sharper when I go up or down stairs."

**Doctor:** "On a scale of zero to ten, how bad is it at its worst?"

**Patient:** "I'd say about a six out of ten."

**Doctor:** "And is it constant, or does it come and go?"

**Patient:** "It's pretty much always there, but it gets worse with activity. Standing for a long time makes it bad too."

**Doctor:** "What about in the morning when you first wake up — any stiffness?"

**Patient:** "Yeah, actually, it's stiff in the morning for about fifteen minutes or so, then it loosens up."

**Doctor:** "Have you noticed any swelling, any locking or giving way?"

**Patient:** "I haven't noticed much swelling myself. No locking or giving way."

**Doctor:** "Have you tried anything for the pain?"

**Patient:** "I've been taking ibuprofen, about four hundred milligrams, and that helps a bit but doesn't take it away completely."

**Doctor:** "Okay. Let me ask about your medical history. Do you have any other medical conditions?"

**Patient:** "I have high blood pressure and type two diabetes."

**Doctor:** "And what medications are you taking for those?"

**Patient:** "Metformin one thousand milligrams twice a day for the diabetes, and lisinopril twenty milligrams once a day for the blood pressure."

**Doctor:** "Any allergies to medications?"

**Patient:** "Yes, I'm allergic to penicillin. I got a rash from it years ago."

**Doctor:** "Good to know. Do you smoke or drink alcohol?"

**Patient:** "No, I don't smoke. I'll have a beer or two on the weekends, nothing excessive."

**Doctor:** "Any family history of joint problems or arthritis?"

**Patient:** "My mother had osteoarthritis in her knees. My father had heart disease."

**Doctor:** "Let me ask a few more questions. Any fevers, any unexplained weight loss recently?"

**Patient:** "No, nothing like that."

**Doctor:** "Any chest pain or shortness of breath?"

**Patient:** "No."

**Doctor:** "Alright, let me examine your knee. I'm going to start with your vitals. Your blood pressure today is one thirty-eight over eighty-two, heart rate seventy-six, temperature ninety-eight point four, and your BMI is twenty-nine point three."

**Doctor:** "You look well, no acute distress. Let me look at your right knee. I don't see any redness or bruising. I do feel a mild effusion here. Let me press along the joint line — tell me if this hurts."

**Patient:** "Ouch, yes, right there on the inside."

**Doctor:** "That's tenderness along the medial joint line. Let me check your range of motion. You can straighten it fully, good. Now bend it — I'm getting about one hundred and twenty degrees on the right compared to one hundred thirty-five on the left. I notice some crepitus with flexion."

**Doctor:** "I'm going to do a couple of special tests now. This is the McMurray test — I'm going to rotate your knee. Does that reproduce the pain?"

**Patient:** "Yes, that's the pain I've been feeling."

**Doctor:** "Positive McMurray on the medial side. Now let me check the Lachman test for your ACL — that feels stable. Anterior and posterior drawer tests are also negative. Varus and valgus stress testing is stable."

**Doctor:** "Let me watch you walk. You have a slightly antalgic gait, favoring the right side. Your sensation is intact and your pulses are good in both feet."

**Doctor:** "Now, I reviewed your X-ray that was done before you came in. It shows some narrowing of the medial joint space and small osteophytes on the medial tibial plateau. No fracture and no loose bodies."

**Doctor:** "So here's what I think is going on. You have osteoarthritis of the right knee, primarily in the medial compartment. Based on the X-ray, I'd classify it as Kellgren-Lawrence Grade Two. Given your history of construction work, this is consistent with occupational loading over the years. The positive McMurray test also raises concern for a possible degenerative medial meniscus tear."

**Doctor:** "Your blood pressure is a little elevated today at one thirty-eight over eighty-two, but I suspect that's related to the pain. And your diabetes sounds stable."

**Doctor:** "For the knee, here's what I'd like to do. First, I want to start you on physical therapy, twice a week for six weeks, focusing on quad strengthening and range of motion. Second, I'm going to prescribe topical diclofenac gel, one percent, apply it three times daily to the right knee. I'd like you to stop the oral ibuprofen since you have diabetes and high blood pressure. If we don't see improvement in six weeks, I'd like to get an MRI to evaluate the meniscus more closely. We should also work on weight management — a goal BMI under twenty-five would help take pressure off the knee. And at your follow-up, we can consider a corticosteroid injection if needed."

**Doctor:** "Continue your lisinopril and metformin as prescribed. We'll recheck your blood pressure at follow-up, and your PCP can check your hemoglobin A1c at your next visit with her."

**Doctor:** "I'd like to see you back in six weeks, or sooner if things get worse. In the meantime, try to avoid prolonged standing and stair climbing when you can. Ice can help after activity. If you develop severe pain, significant swelling, or your knee locks up, go to urgent care. Any questions?"

**Patient:** "No, that all makes sense. Thank you, doctor."

**Doctor:** "You're welcome. Take care."
